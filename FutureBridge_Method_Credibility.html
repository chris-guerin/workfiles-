FutureBridge Signal Engine · The Method

This method is not new. It is three established academic disciplines wired together and made operational with live signals.

Discovery-driven strategy, causal modelling, and driver-based planning are each well established in academic and commercial strategy practice. FutureBridge did not invent them. What is different is connecting all three into a single system that updates continuously as real-world events arrive — and links that directly to decisions, not to reports.
Most approaches describe the future. This approach tests whether it is actually arriving.
The academic foundations
Three established disciplines — one connected system
PILLAR 01
What must be true
Discovery-driven planning
Academic anchor
Rita McGrath · Ian MacMillan
Harvard Business Review, 1995. Columbia Business School. Foundational in corporate venture strategy and new market entry.
The established concept
Strategy for uncertain markets should begin with explicit assumptions about what must be true for success — not treat those assumptions as facts. Making them visible enables deliberate testing and faster learning when conditions change.
FutureBridge application
We define those assumptions as measurable thresholds — WNTBT (What Needs to Be True) — with specific numbers, not narratives. Each threshold is a falsifiable claim the evidence either supports or doesn't.
PILLAR 02
How the system works
System dynamics and causal modelling
Academic anchor
Jay W. Forrester
MIT Sloan School of Management. Originated system dynamics modelling. Basis for much of modern supply chain, macroeconomic, and policy simulation thinking.
The established concept
Complex outcomes are driven by interconnected variables and feedback loops — not single causes. Understanding a system means mapping the causal structure, not just observing outputs. Changes in one node propagate through the whole.
FutureBridge application
We map the causal structure of each strategic question — regulation, technology cost, supply chain, competitive dynamics — so we know which variables actually drive the outcome and in what sequence.
PILLAR 03
What moves the outcome
Driver trees and driver-based planning
Academic anchor
McKinsey & Company · Corporate FP&A practice
McKinsey driver trees, widely adopted in CFO and FP&A (Financial Planning and Analysis) planning. Decomposes business performance into measurable input variables.
The established concept
Business performance is driven by a small number of measurable input variables. Managing the business means tracking those inputs — not just the financial outputs. The right drivers, correctly measured, give early warning of where outcomes are heading.
FutureBridge application
We track the specific input nodes — tariff rates, local content percentages, cell costs, regulatory milestones — that drive the strategic outcome. External news events are classified by which node they move and by how much.
What FutureBridge adds
The components are standard. The connection is not.
Each of the three disciplines above exists in isolation across strategy consulting and academic practice. Discovery-driven planning rarely gets connected to a real causal model. Driver trees rarely get connected to external signal classification. And none of them typically update in real time as market events occur. FutureBridge connects all three — and adds a fourth layer: live news classified against the model, updating the thresholds continuously rather than at the next strategy review.
What the connection enables
A tariff announcement, a technology cost milestone, or a regulatory vote is classified against the causal model immediately. The relevant WNTBT threshold updates. The decision window opens or closes. That cycle runs continuously — not annually.
What it replaces
Strategy reviews that are obsolete by the time they are presented. Trend reports that describe what happened but do not say what it means for a specific decision. Scenario planning that produces options but no thresholds for choosing between them.
The operational flow — from evidence to decision
01
Hypothesis
A falsifiable claim with a specific threshold and the decision it informs. Not a belief — a statement that can be proven wrong.
→
02
WNTBT thresholds
The specific conditions — with numbers — that must hold for the hypothesis to be valid. Each one is a measurable claim, not a narrative assumption.
→
03
Causal driver map
The input variables that determine whether each threshold is met — technology cost, regulatory timing, supply chain depth, market dynamics. Mapped as a causal system, not a list.
→
04
Live signal classification
External news is classified by which driver node it moves and in which direction. Only events that affect a WNTBT threshold become signals. Everything else is noise.
→
05
Decision window
When multiple thresholds converge — or when one crosses — the decision window opens. The system surfaces this directly. No translation layer required.

If someone asks: "Is this a new framework?"

The honest answer: no. It is built on Rita McGrath's discovery-driven planning, Forrester's system dynamics, and McKinsey-style driver trees. These are not proprietary. What is different is connecting them into a system that updates continuously with external signals and makes the decision window explicit.

That is the answer that earns credibility. Claiming a proprietary method invites scepticism. Naming the established foundations does the opposite.

Part I — The Global MapWho Has Sovereign Cheap Electricity — and Who Never Will

The single most important variable in long-term electricity cost is whether a country owns amortised, dispatchable, zero-fuel-cost generation. In practice, this means hydropower built 30–70 years ago, fully paid off, with marginal generation cost of €0.005–0.01/kWh. Eight countries have this. Everyone else is paying 4–10× more and always will be — unless something fundamentally changes.

The Benchmark to Beat

Norway's existing hydro fleet: 90% of electricity, built 1950s–80s, publicly owned, fully amortised. Marginal cost: €0.005–0.01/kWh. Dispatchable on demand. Lasts 50–100+ years. Zero carbon. Zero fuel cost. Water flows downhill through a turbine paid for decades ago. This is what cheap electricity looks like — and it's what every other country is measured against.

The Verdict

Nothing beats amortised hydro before 2050. One thing might by 2075. Enhanced geothermal is the only technology that offers the same combination of dispatchability + zero fuel cost + multi-decade asset life that makes hydro special. If EGS follows the shale drilling cost curve and reaches $0.03/kWh by the 2040s, and those plants operate 30+ years approaching amortisation, then by the 2070s Germany and the UK could have "near-free" dispatchable green power.

For any boardroom decision with a horizon shorter than 2050: the Tier 1 sovereignty map is fixed. Those 8 countries will have the cheapest electricity on Earth for the next 25 years, and no investment, policy, or technology changes that. The question for everyone else is how close they can get.

Part II — The UK Specific CaseWhy the UK Chose Offshore Wind (and Why It's Not Enough)

The UK has no hydro to build, no nuclear fleet in production, no geothermal industry. It does have the best offshore wind resource on Earth. So it played the only card in its hand — and it's not a bad card. But wind alone cannot deliver cheap consumer electricity, for four structural reasons.

It's Not a Bad Card

North Sea and Scottish waters have the strongest, most consistent winds on Earth — UK offshore capacity factors of 40–50% are among the best globally. AR7 offshore wind strike price of £91/MWh is 38% cheaper than new-build gas at £147/MWh. Wind farms reduced UK wholesale prices by roughly a third in 2025 — without existing wind, wholesale would be ~£121/MWh instead of ~£83/MWh. And wind is domestic in a country that became a net gas importer in 2004.

But Wind Alone = The Denmark Trap

Denmark has 58% wind penetration — among the highest in the world. Yet Danish consumer electricity costs €0.35/kWh — the 4th highest in Europe. Cheap generation ≠ cheap consumer electricity. Denmark built world-class wind but failed to translate it into cheap consumer prices because of taxation, grid costs, backup requirements, and market design. The UK is walking the exact same path.

04 — The Four-Layer Price TrapWhy Cheap Wind Never Reaches UK Consumers

UK offshore wind generates at ~£50–60/MWh. UK consumers pay ~£245/MWh (24.5p/kWh). The gap isn't theft — it's system design. Four layers reprice cheap wind at expensive gas rates before it reaches the wall socket.

LayerMechanismCost ImpactCan Be Fixed?
1. Marginal Pricing Gas (last unit needed) sets price for ALL generation. Even when 50% is wind, the last 1% from gas sets the wholesale price. Gas set UK price 98% of the time in 2023 vs 7% in France. +£30–40/MWh Yes — market reform
2. Intermittency Backup Must maintain ~25 GW gas backup for calm periods. Plants paid to exist (Capacity Market £65/kW/yr) even when running at low utilisation. Curtailment rising: 10 TWh wasted in 2025. +£15–25/MWh Partly — needs storage
3. Network Costs Wind in Scotland/North Sea, demand in London/South. £58bn transmission investment in NESO Clean Power 2030 plan. Network charges ~25% of consumer bill and rising. +£40–60/MWh Structural — geography fixed
4. Interconnector Coupling 8.4 GW links to EU. When French nuclear offline or German gas expensive, UK prices rise by contagion. Financial traders arbitrage differentials. Net imports 37 TWh (10% demand). +£10–30/MWh Yes — policy choice
Result Wind LCOE ~£50–60/MWh → Consumer price ~£245/MWh 4× markup Fixable with political will

The Irony

Norway's sovereign wealth fund received £5.9bn in UK gas profits in 2024 alone. Norway built publicly-owned hydro with public money in the 1950s–80s. The UK privatised its electricity system in 1990. The country that did energy right profits from the country that did energy wrong.

06 — The Seven FixesWhat the UK Needs, What It Costs, and How to Fund It

Every recommendation is funded by redirecting money already in the system — from waste, windfall profits, or market redesign. No new taxes required. Total programme cost: £20–26bn over 10 years. Total money currently being wasted or extracted: £30–49bn per year.

Priority 1 — Urgent

Build Scotland's Pumped Storage Hydro

"UK has spent £85bn on wind since 2014 and £0 on storage. Like buying a Ferrari and refusing to buy tyres."

11 schemes across Scotland and Wales, combined 10 GW / 200 GWh — a five-fold increase in UK long-duration storage. Technology is proven (Dinorwig has run since 1984). Sites surveyed, planning consents granted. Only blocker: a Cap & Floor revenue framework the privatised market won't provide. Coire Glas (SSE, 1.3 GW, £2bn) is the flagship — FID expected early 2026 pending Cap & Floor award.

Priority 2 — Urgent

Reform the Wholesale Market

"Stop paying champagne prices for tap water."

UCL's "Green Power Pool": split market in two. Renewables and nuclear sell at average cost (~£45–55/MWh) through long-term contracts. Gas sells at marginal cost in a separate backup market. Consumers access cheap clean power directly.

"Stop Morgan Stanley setting your electricity bill."

Priority 6 — Strategic Gamechanger

UK FORGE — Enhanced Geothermal R&D

"The only technology that gives UK what Norway has: dispatchable, zero-fuel, sovereign, permanent."

Cornwall proposed UK FORGE programme (December 2025). Fervo (US) drills 4.8km in 16 days; UK takes 100 days for 3.5km. If UK transfers Fervo's techniques to Cornish granite, Cornwall alone could deliver 4 GW electricity + 13 GW heat. Over 15 years (2025–2040): £80–170bn transferred from UK consumers to shareholders, foreign governments, and financial traders — money that could have built sovereign infrastructure lasting 100+ years.

The Bottom Line

The UK doesn't have an energy cost problem. It has an energy design problem. It generates increasingly cheap wind power, then reprices it at gas rates, wastes £1.8bn/yr paying wind farms to switch off, lets financial traders arbitrage interconnectors, and channels £30bn/yr to foreign governments and US asset managers — while 4 million households sit in energy debt.

The total cost to permanently fix this is £20–26bn over 10 years — less than one year's energy sector profits, less than two years of system waste. The assets built then operate for 50–100+ years.

Norway built publicly-owned hydro with public money in the 1950s–80s and now enjoys near-free electricity forever. The UK can build publicly-backed pumped storage, SMR nuclear, and geothermal with money already being wasted in its own system — and close half the gap within 15 years.

The money is there. The engineering is proven. The sites are surveyed. The only thing missing is a government willing to choose its citizens over its market ideology.

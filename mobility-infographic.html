04
Direction of Travel to 2030
↓

Raw energy (BEV)

RE LCOE still declining 3-5%/yr. Solar + wind overbuild compresses wholesale power.

↑

Refining margin (ICE)

Refinery closures in EU tighten product supply. Structural margin expansion.

↑↑

Networks (BEV)

Massive grid upgrade cycle 2025-2035. DSOs raising tariffs 20-40% across EU. THE big cost driver.

↑↑

Govt taxes (BEV)

Fuel duty revenue declining → govts must tax EVs. Road pricing emerging by 2028-30.

↑↑

Policy costs (ICE)

EU ETS2 from 2027 adds €0.05-0.10/L on transport fuel. ICE gets more expensive.

↓

CPO margins (public)

Competition intensifies. Utilisation improves. Margins compress toward utility returns from 2028.

↑

ICE distribution cost

Falling volumes = higher per-litre forecourt overhead. Death spiral for marginal stations.

→

Oil commodity (ICE)

Range-bound $65-85/bbl. Neither shortage nor glut scenario dominant.

Net effect: ICE cost/km rises 10-20% by 2030. BEV home stays flat (generation gains offset by network + tax). BEV public falls 10-15% as CPO scale improves. The gap widens in favour of BEV everywhere except where network costs explode.

05
Key Insights

Network operators capture the marginal euro

31% → 40%+

DSOs/TSOs are the single biggest winner from electrification. In Germany, networks already take €0.021/km — and this share is rising everywhere as grid reinforcement costs pass through.

Cheap generation ≠ cheap mobility

Germany's wholesale power fell 45% from 2022 peaks. Household prices barely moved. Network charges, taxes, and margins absorbed the savings. Cheap generation is necessary but not sufficient.

€200bn+ fiscal hole by 2035

6:1

UK govt takes €0.056/km from petrol drivers, €0.009/km from home-charging BEV. That 6:1 tax ratio is politically unsustainable. Per-km road pricing is the only scalable answer.

Public charging is a luxury good

UK BEV public charging (€0.131/km) is more expensive than diesel (€0.099/km). EV economics break for drivers without home charging — typically renters and the least affluent.

CPO margins = the new refining margins

Public charging operators capture 30-65% of consumer BEV cost as margin + infrastructure recovery. Higher than refiners ever achieved. Will compress, but first-movers are rewarded.

The US is a different planet

At €0.054/km gasoline and €0.028/km BEV, the absolute saving is ~€300/yr for 12,000km. The economic case for EVs is 3-4× weaker than in Europe.

06
Three Bold Predictions
Prediction 01

By 2030, the UK's largest CPO will be acquired by a regulated utility — not an oil major

The UK's extreme public charging margin is structurally a grid-edge distribution business. Regulated utilities (SSE, National Grid, Iberdrola) can cross-subsidise connections and earn regulated returns. Oil majors will exit once CPO ROIC converges to 6-8% utility returns rather than the 15%+ they model.

Prediction 02

Germany will introduce an "electricity autobahn surcharge" — a per-kWh grid fee for EV charging — before 2029

Network costs represent 31% of BEV cost/km and are rising at 8-10%/yr. Political pressure to shield households while funding €100bn+ in transmission upgrades will force segmentation. EV charging will be classified differently — effectively recreating fuel duty by another name, routed through DSOs.

Prediction 03

Norway pioneers GPS-based road pricing by 2027 — the template for all of Europe

Norway's €0.019/km is artificially low. Toll revenue is cratering as EVs reach 90%+ of new sales. The government is already consulting on per-km charges linked to GPS tracking. Norway will be the first to price EVs at parity with ICE running costs — not because electricity gets expensive, but because the roads need paying for.
